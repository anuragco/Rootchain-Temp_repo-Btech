Subsistence Farming

Small-scale farming where farmers grow crops primarily for their own consumption rather than for sale. It relies on traditional methods, minimal inputs, and is common in rural areas where families sustain themselves with basic agricultural produce. Farmers use indigenous seeds and organic manure, often depending on rainfall for irrigation.

About

Organic Farming

A chemical-free farming technique that relies on natural fertilizers like compost and manure, crop rotation, and biological pest control. It enhances soil health, produces healthier food, and is gaining popularity in states like Sikkim, which is fully organic. It also reduces environmental pollution and improves biodiversity, ensuring long-term soil fertility.

About

Mixed Farming

A system where crops and livestock are raised together on the same farm, providing multiple sources of income. It improves land utilization, soil fertility, and financial security, as farmers benefit from both crop yields and dairy, poultry, or fishery products. This integrated approach reduces risks and ensures continuous productivity throughout the year.

About

Commercial Farming

Large-scale farming aimed at producing crops for sale in domestic and international markets. It involves high investments in modern technology, fertilizers, pesticides, and irrigation systems to maximize yields, commonly practiced for cash crops like wheat, rice, and cotton. This method requires skilled labor, mechanization, and a well-developed supply chain for market distribution.

About

Crop Rotation

The practice of growing different types of crops sequentially in the same field to maintain soil fertility, control pests, and reduce soil depletion. Common rotations in India include cereals, legumes, and oilseeds, improving yield sustainability over time. This method also minimizes the risk of soil-borne diseases and enhances long-term land productivity.

About

Zero Budget Natural Farming

A low-cost, chemical-free farming method that uses cow dung, urine, and natural bio-pesticides instead of expensive fertilizers and pesticides. Popularized in Andhra Pradesh and Karnataka, ZBNF promotes sustainable agriculture and improves soil fertility. It helps reduce dependency on market inputs while enhancing biodiversity and farmer profitability.

About

Shifting Cultivation

Practiced in northeastern India, this method involves clearing forests, cultivating crops for a few years, and then abandoning the land to let it regenerate. It is a traditional but unsustainable method leading to deforestation and soil degradation. Farmers use slash-and-burn techniques, which temporarily improve soil fertility but eventually reduce long-term productivity.

About

Dryland Farming

Practiced in areas with low rainfall, such as Rajasthan and parts of Maharashtra, this method uses drought-resistant crops like millets and pulses. It involves water conservation techniques, minimal irrigation, and soil management practices to sustain agriculture in arid regions. Farmers use deep plowing, mulching, and rainwater harvesting to enhance soil moisture retention.

About

Irrigated Farming

Relies on artificial irrigation methods like canals, wells, and drip irrigation to ensure continuous crop production. It is common in Punjab, Haryana, and Tamil Nadu, where a stable water supply enables the cultivation of high-yield crops like paddy, wheat, and sugarcane. Advanced irrigation systems like sprinklers and fertigation help conserve water while boosting productivity.

About

Intensive Farming

A high-input farming system that uses advanced techniques, chemical fertilizers, hybrid seeds, and mechanization to maximize production per unit of land. This method is common in densely populated areas where land availability is limited but food demand is high. It focuses on achieving high yields through scientific methods but may lead to soil depletion over time.

About

Extensive Farming

Involves cultivating large land areas with minimal inputs and lower yields per hectare. It is commonly practiced in regions with abundant land but low population density, such as parts of Madhya Pradesh and Rajasthan, mainly for grains, oilseeds, and pulses. This method is labor-efficient but less productive per unit area compared to intensive farming.

About

Horticulture Farming

Focuses on the cultivation of fruits, vegetables, flowers, and medicinal plants. It is highly profitable and practiced in states like Maharashtra, Karnataka, and Himachal Pradesh. Horticulture farming supports exports and enhances the nutritional diversity of agricultural produce. It also includes greenhouse cultivation, vertical gardening, and controlled environment farming.

About

Plantation Farming

Large-scale commercial farming of cash crops like tea, coffee, rubber, and spices. It requires high labor and investment and is practiced in Kerala, Assam, Tamil Nadu, and Karnataka. These crops are often grown for export and contribute significantly to India’s economy. Plantation farming supports employment and involves scientific soil management techniques for sustainability.

About

Aquaponics

A modern, sustainable method that combines aquaculture (fish farming) with hydroponics (growing plants without soil). Fish waste provides nutrients for plants, and plants filter the water for fish. This eco-friendly system is gaining popularity in urban and water-scarce regions. It minimizes waste, reduces land dependency, and ensures year-round food production.

About

Permaculture Farming

A sustainable farming approach that mimics natural ecosystems by integrating crops, animals, and soil conservation techniques. It emphasizes biodiversity, organic farming, and minimal external inputs, ensuring long-term soil fertility and resilience. Practiced in various Indian states, permaculture reduces waste, conserves water, and enhances self-sufficiency for small-scale farmers. This method also focuses on sustainable land management, promoting natural pest control and mixed cropping.

About

Hydroponics

A soil-less farming technique where plants grow in nutrient-rich water solutions. It allows farming in urban areas and regions with poor soil quality. This technique is efficient, requires less water, and enables year-round cultivation of crops like lettuce, herbs, and tomatoes. It is widely used in greenhouses and vertical farms for sustainable production.

About

Agroforestry

Integrates trees with crops or livestock farming to improve biodiversity, enhance soil conservation, and provide additional income. Common in Karnataka, Odisha, and Madhya Pradesh, this method helps farmers combat climate change while generating timber, fruits, and fodder. Agroforestry also reduces carbon footprint and supports ecosystem restoration.

About

Precision Farming

Uses modern technologies like GPS, sensors, and data analytics to optimize input usage and increase crop productivity. It minimizes waste, reduces costs, and enhances efficiency, helping farmers make informed decisions about irrigation, fertilization, and pest control. Precision farming also supports automation, reducing labor dependency and improving accuracy.

About

Vertical Farming

A modern technique of growing crops in stacked layers, often indoors or in urban areas. It saves space, reduces water consumption, and enables year-round production of vegetables, herbs, and microgreens, making it ideal for high-density cities like Mumbai and Delhi. This method enhances urban food security and reduces transportation-related carbon emissions.

About

Sericulture

The practice of rearing silkworms for silk production, mainly in Karnataka, Andhra Pradesh, and West Bengal. Farmers cultivate mulberry plants to feed silkworms, which produce high-quality silk, contributing significantly to India’s textile and export industries. Sericulture provides rural employment and supports India's traditional silk weaving industry.
